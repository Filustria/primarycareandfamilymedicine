A Cost-effectiveness analysis of the Family Medicine Vocational trainning initiative for capacity building during the Rio de Janeiro Primary Health Care Reform

Introduction

Summary and Objectives

This is a longitudinal retrospective study with the main objective of measuring the impact and the cost-effectiveness of vocational training in Family Medicine as part of the capacity building policy for Primary Health Care (PHC) implemented in 2012 during the PHC reform that happened between 2009 to 2016 in the city of Rio de Janeiro.

Since 1994 Brazil has been experiencing a big reform in PHC and today almost 70% of the Brazilian population is covered by the Programa Saúde da Família (PSF - Family Health Program). This Federal policy stablishes a structure for PHC at the municipal level, providing financial resources for a Family Health Team (ESF - Equipe de Saúde da Família) formed by one Physician, one Nurse and 4 to 6 Community Health Agents to take care of 4000 people living inside a catchment area.

Rio de Janeiro was the last Brazilian capital to embrace the PSF. A PHC reform took place in the city between 2008 and 2016 and during this period the coverage increased from only 3.5% to 70%. 1400 Family Health Teams are currently working in the city, providing care for more than 3.5 million people in a system that merges public funding with private non-for-profit organizations responsible for management, care delivery and for structural and human resources.

In 2012 the Health Department started a strategy for human resources capacity building in Family Medicine signing a partnership with the two Family Medicine residency programs stablished at that time in the city and also launching a new residency program. The strategy not only expanded the number of residency vacancies (210 vacancies in 2018) in Family Medicine (FM), but also included a financial incentive for both preceptors and residents, making FM more attractive to doctors seeking a specialization. This strategy aimed not only at capacity building of human resources, but also at provision and fixation of doctors in Primary Care Clinics in Rio de Janeiro.

Despite the recent efforts of the Rio de Janeiro health department to make Primary Care more effective – establishing new clinics, improving working conditions and training new specialists – they were not enough to change policy makers mindset about the importance of PC and the need for professional training. Common generalizations still prevail among them, perpetuating wrong notions such as no need for specific training, other than medical graduation, to work in PC.

This initiative ended up creating a natural experiment in the city. Some clinics now have only Family Physicians as medical health care providers, some have different combinations of trained Family Physicians and generalists and some have no trained doctors at all. In this scenario, there is a clear difference in access to health care and in the way patients are treated that is due to the amount of training received. These differences weren’t measured until now and “what is the actual difference?” – i.e., the real effect that having specific medical training focused on PC – it is an important question to be addressed, especially for countries with young PHC systems.

The main objective of this thesis is to assess the impact on health outcomes of Family Medicine training provided by residency programs and perform a cost-effectiveness analysis of the investment made.

Methodology

Databases

This research is composed by a cohort of patients registered and attended at the PHC clinics in the Rio de Janeiro municipality. The study will use data from (1) the electronic medical records (EMR) to conform the study population between the years 2010 and 2018, (2) data from the National Registry of Health Establishments (CNES) between the years 2010 and 2018, (3) data from the Mortality Information System (SIM) between 2000 and 2018, (4) data on referrals requested for patients to Secondary Care between 2010 and 2018 (SISREG platform); and (5) data from the Hospital Admissions National System (SISAIH) between 2008 and 2018, which compiles the information on hospital admissions through the registration of the Hospital Admission Authorizations (AIH). These five databases will be merged using a linkage process via with RecordLinkage and fastLink packages in R.

Population study and Exposure

The reference population for this study will be composed of the patients enrolled in the Primary Care clinics. Patients who have consulted in this period, but who are not registered in the health units will also be counted as the base population. The exposures of interest in the study are the medical professionals providing care to this population, representing different levels of training, i.e., (1) Generalists providing PC, with no special training; (2) generalists linked to the “Programa Mais Médicos” (PMM), a Federal policy for provision and fixation of doctors in remote areas; (3) trained Family Physicians; (4) Preceptors in Family Medicine; and (5) Residents in Family Medicine. Each of these five groups of professionals is linked to the service under a different employment regime, receiving different salaries, from different paying sources and with different financial incentives, conforming five different forms of medical work in PHC.

The five groups will be summarized into two main categories - Family Physicians (FP) and non-Family Physicians (generalists) and every Family Health Care Team (ESF) will be assigned as having one of the two categories. The original division is necessary to compute the cost-effectiveness analysis that will be held later. The outcomes that will be studied will result in three different articles, each of them addressing a distinct FMdomain of practice.

Description of the three articles

Ambulatory-Care Sensitive Conditions

Longitudinal analysis through a Panel Data study, comparing FP to generalists for the measurement of outcomes related to the Brazilian list of Ambulatory-Care Sensitive Conditions (ACSC) [1]. This list comprises 20 domains covering infectious conditions (pneumonia and bacterial infections), chronic health problems (angina, heart failure) and preventive health care procedures (prenatal care), providing a good framework to approach the FM broad scope of practice. Rates of hospital admissions, referrals to secondary care, drugs prescribed, and laboratory exams ordered will be measured for every ESF. The Incidence Rate Ratio will be calculated between the two categories, estimating the interventions avoided (hospital admissions, referrals, drugs prescription and labs ordered) and, finally, the expenses avoided by FP in comparison to generalists. The unit of analysis will be the rate of each intervention by every ESF to those patients attended for ACSC. For the Economic Analysis, this study will measure the cost for avoiding the interventions mentioned above for those patients attended for ACSC.

Comprehensive care for patients with multimorbidity

Survival analysis of patients with Multimorbidity treated by FP versus generalists. Multimorbidity, defined as the occurrence of two or more chronic conditions in the same patient, will be measured for every patient attended at the PC clinics and time to event will be measured from the moment the patient fits the definition of Multimorbidity to the occurrence of two outcomes: death from any cause and first hospital admission. A longitudinal analysis using a Cox regression model to estimate the survival of multimorbid patients followed in Rio de Janeiro’s Primary Care, comparing patients treated by FP to generalists. The Charlson Index for comorbidity will be used to adjust the model, allowing patients with different burden of diseases to be compared. A Cost-effectiveness analysis will measure DALYs averted among multimorbid patients treated by FP compared to generalists.

Survival of patients at cardiovascular risk

Survival analysis comparing trained FP to generalists when dealing with patients at risk for cardiovascular outcomes. Patients older than 55 years-old with a cardiovascular risk greater than 10% in 10 years, according to the Framingham score, will be included in the study and time to event will be measured. A longitudinal analysis using a Cox regression model to estimate the survival for those patients treated at the Rio de Janeiro’s PHC System, comparing patients treated by FP to generalists. Individual cardiovascular risk will be used to adjust the model the same way the Charlson Index for comorbidity, allowing comparisons between patients with different burden of diseases. The events of interest are the main outcomes related to cardiovascular morbimortality, i.e., myocardial infarction, angina, heart failure, stroke, transient ischemic attack, coronary revascularization, claudication, and CHD and non-CHD mortality. A Cost-effectiveness analysis will measure DALYs averted among patients treated by treated by FP compared to generalists.
